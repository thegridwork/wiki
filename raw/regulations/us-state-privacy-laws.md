## Individual State Details

### Virginia — Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (VCDPA)
**Effective:** January 1, 2023
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in VA or target VA residents AND control/process PI of >= 100,000 consumers/year, OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive > 50% gross revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- No private right of action
- Opt-in consent required for processing sensitive data (CCPA allows opt-out to limit use)
- No revenue-based applicability threshold
- Data protection assessments required for targeted advertising, sale of PI, profiling, sensitive data processing
- Right to appeal a controller's refusal

### Colorado — Colorado Privacy Act (CPA)
**Effective:** July 1, 2023
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in CO or target CO residents AND control/process PI of >= 100,000 consumers/year, OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Universal opt-out mechanism required (July 1, 2024)
- Recognizes "bona fide loyalty programs" with specific protections
- Data protection assessments required
- Consent required for sensitive data processing
- Affirmative opt-in for processing data of known children (under 13)

### Connecticut — Connecticut Data Privacy Act (CTDPA)
**Effective:** July 1, 2023
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in CT or target CT residents AND control/process PI of >= 100,000 consumers/year, OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive > 25% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Lower revenue-from-sale threshold (25% vs 50%)
- Consent required for sensitive data processing
- Universal opt-out mechanism required (January 1, 2025)
- Recognizes Global Privacy Control signals
- Cure period sunsets December 31, 2024

### Utah — Utah Consumer Privacy Act (UCPA)
**Effective:** December 31, 2023
**Enforced by:** Attorney General (Division of Consumer Protection investigates)
**Applicability:** Entities with >= $25,000,000 annual revenue AND conduct business in UT or target UT residents AND meet EITHER >= 100,000 consumers/year OR >= 25,000 consumers + > 50% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Requires both revenue threshold AND data volume threshold (most restrictive)
- No right to correct
- No data protection assessment requirement
- No universal opt-out mechanism
- Narrower sensitive data definition (no precise geolocation)

### Iowa — Iowa Consumer Data Protection Act (ICDPA)
**Effective:** January 1, 2025
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in IA or target IA residents AND control/process PI of >= 100,000 consumers/year, OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive > 50% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- 90-day cure period (longest among state laws, permanent)
- No right to correct
- No right to opt out of profiling
- No data protection assessment requirement
- Narrower — most business-friendly state privacy law

### Indiana — Indiana Consumer Data Protection Act (INCDPA)
**Effective:** January 1, 2026
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in IN or target IN residents AND control/process PI of >= 100,000 consumers/year, OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive > 50% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Closely modeled on Virginia VCDPA
- 30-day cure period (sunsets January 1, 2028)
- Consent for sensitive data processing
- Data protection assessments required

### Tennessee — Tennessee Information Protection Act (TIPA)
**Effective:** July 1, 2025
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in TN or produce products/services targeting TN residents AND exceed $25,000,000 revenue AND control/process PI of >= 175,000 consumers/year, OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive > 50% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Higher consumer data threshold (175,000 vs 100,000)
- Requires $25M revenue floor
- Affirmative defense for maintaining and complying with a privacy program conforming to NIST frameworks
- 60-day cure period (permanent)

### Montana — Montana Consumer Data Privacy Act (MCDPA)
**Effective:** October 1, 2024
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in MT or target MT residents AND control/process PI of >= 50,000 consumers/year (lower threshold due to small population), OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive > 50% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Lowest consumer threshold (50,000) reflecting small state population
- Universal opt-out required (January 1, 2025)
- Consent for sensitive data processing
- Data protection assessments required
- Cure period sunsets April 1, 2026

### Texas — Texas Data Privacy and Security Act (TDPSA)
**Effective:** July 1, 2024
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in TX or produce products/services consumed by TX residents AND process PI AND are not a small business (as defined by SBA). No specific numerical threshold.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- No specific revenue or data volume threshold — applies to all non-small businesses
- Broadest applicability of any state law
- Universal opt-out required (January 1, 2025)
- 30-day cure period (permanent)
- Data protection assessments required
- AG can seek $7,500 per violation

### Oregon — Oregon Consumer Privacy Act (OCPA)
**Effective:** July 1, 2024
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in OR or target OR residents AND control/process PI of >= 100,000 consumers/year, OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive >= 25% revenue from sale of PI. Nonprofits included (effective July 1, 2025).
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Applies to nonprofits (rare among state privacy laws)
- Lower revenue-from-sale threshold (25%)
- Right to obtain a list of specific third parties to whom PI was disclosed
- Universal opt-out required (January 1, 2026)
- Expanded definition of sensitive data includes transgender/nonbinary status

### Delaware — Delaware Personal Data Privacy Act (DPDPA)
**Effective:** January 1, 2025
**Enforced by:** Attorney General (Department of Justice)
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in DE or target DE residents AND control/process PI of >= 35,000 consumers/year (excluding payment transactions), OR >= 10,000 consumers AND derive > 20% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Very low thresholds (35,000 / 10,000 consumers, 20% revenue)
- Broad applicability despite small state population
- Consent for sensitive data processing
- Universal opt-out required (January 1, 2026)
- Cure period sunsets December 31, 2025

### New Jersey — New Jersey Data Privacy Act (NJDPA)
**Effective:** January 15, 2025
**Enforced by:** Attorney General (Division of Consumer Affairs)
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in NJ or target NJ residents AND control/process PI of >= 100,000 consumers/year (excluding payment transactions), OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- No percentage threshold for revenue from sale (any revenue qualifies)
- Financial data partially covered (unlike most states that exempt GLBA data entirely)
- Consent for sensitive data processing
- Universal opt-out required (July 15, 2025)
- Heightened protections for health data beyond HIPAA scope

### New Hampshire — NH SB 255
**Effective:** January 1, 2025
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in NH or target NH residents AND control/process PI of >= 35,000 consumers/year (excluding payment transactions), OR >= 10,000 consumers AND derive > 25% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Modeled closely on Connecticut CTDPA
- Low consumer threshold (35,000)
- Consent for sensitive data processing
- 60-day cure period (permanent)

### Nebraska — Nebraska Data Privacy Act (NDPA)
**Effective:** January 1, 2025
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in NE or target NE residents AND are not small businesses (SBA definition). No specific numerical threshold.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- No numeric consumer/revenue threshold (similar to Texas approach)
- Universal opt-out required (January 1, 2026)
- Consent for sensitive data processing
- 30-day cure period sunsets January 1, 2026

### Maryland — Maryland Online Data Privacy Act (MODPA)
**Effective:** October 1, 2025
**Enforced by:** Attorney General (Division of Consumer Protection)
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in MD or target MD residents AND control/process PI of >= 35,000 consumers/year (excluding payment transactions), OR >= 10,000 consumers AND derive > 20% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Strongest data minimization requirement — may only process PI that is "reasonably necessary and proportionate" (stricter than CPRA)
- No cure period (strictest enforcement)
- Prohibits sale of sensitive data entirely (not just opt-out)
- Prohibits targeted advertising to minors (under 18)
- Requires consent for processing sensitive data
- Universal opt-out required (October 1, 2025)

### Minnesota — Minnesota Consumer Data Privacy Act (MCDPA)
**Effective:** July 31, 2025
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in MN or target MN residents AND control/process PI of >= 100,000 consumers/year, OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive > 25% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Right to question the result of automated profiling decisions
- Data protection assessments required
- Consent for sensitive data processing
- Universal opt-out required (July 31, 2026)
- 30-day cure period sunsets July 31, 2026
- Includes right to data portability in a commonly used format

### Kentucky — Kentucky Consumer Data Protection Act (KCDPA)
**Effective:** January 1, 2026
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in KY or target KY residents AND control/process PI of >= 100,000 consumers/year, OR >= 25,000 consumers AND derive > 50% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Closely modeled on Virginia VCDPA
- 30-day cure period (sunsets January 1, 2028)
- Consent for sensitive data processing
- Data protection assessments required

### Rhode Island — Rhode Island Data Transparency and Privacy Protection Act (RIDPA)
**Effective:** January 1, 2026
**Enforced by:** Attorney General
**Applicability:** Entities that conduct business in RI or target RI residents AND control/process PI of >= 35,000 consumers/year (excluding payment transactions), OR >= 10,000 consumers AND derive > 20% revenue from sale of PI.
**Key differences from CCPA:**
- Low thresholds (similar to Delaware)
- 30-day cure period (sunsets January 1, 2028)
- Consent for sensitive data processing
- Requires reasonable data security practices

## Common Consumer Rights Across States

| Right | CA | VA | CO | CT | UT | TX | OR | MD |
|-------|----|----|----|----|----|----|----|----|
| Access/Know | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y |
| Delete | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y |
| Correct | Y | Y | Y | Y | N | Y | Y | Y |
| Portability | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y |
| Opt-out of sale | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y |
| Opt-out of targeted ads | N* | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y | Y |
| Opt-out of profiling | N* | Y | Y | Y | N | Y | Y | Y |
| Limit sensitive PI use | Y | N** | N** | N** | N** | N** | N** | N** |
| Appeal | N | Y | Y | Y | N | Y | Y | Y |

*California addresses via "sharing" opt-out and sensitive PI limitation.
**These states require opt-in consent for sensitive data processing rather than an opt-out limitation right.

## Enforcement Comparison

- **Strictest:** Maryland (no cure period, prohibits sale of sensitive data)
- **Broadest applicability:** Texas, Nebraska (no numeric threshold, applies to all non-small businesses)
- **Most business-friendly:** Iowa (90-day permanent cure, fewest consumer rights), Utah (requires revenue + data thresholds)
- **Strongest profiling protections:** Minnesota (right to question automated decisions), Colorado
- **Nonprofit coverage:** Oregon (only state to include nonprofits)
