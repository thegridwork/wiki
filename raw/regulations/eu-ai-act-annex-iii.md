# EU AI Act — Annex III: High-Risk AI System Areas
## Complete Reference (Article 6(2), Article 7)

Annex III lists the areas in which AI systems are classified as high-risk under Article 6(2). The Commission may amend this list via delegated acts under Article 7. Systems in these areas are subject to the full high-risk obligations (Articles 8-15, 16-29, 40-49) unless the Article 6(3) exception applies (system does not pose significant risk to health, safety, or fundamental rights).

**Application date**: 2 August 2026

---

## Area 1: Biometrics

**Governing articles**: Articles 6(2), 5(1)(d)-(h), 26, 50(3)

Insofar as their use is not prohibited under Article 5.

### 1(a) — Remote biometric identification systems
AI systems intended for remote biometric identification of natural persons, excluding biometric verification systems that confirm a person is who they claim to be.

**Examples**:
- Facial recognition systems matching faces against a watchlist database
- Gait recognition systems identifying individuals at a distance
- Voice identification systems matching voice prints against a reference database
- Multi-modal biometric identification combining face, iris, and fingerprint

### 1(b) — Biometric categorisation systems
AI systems intended for biometric categorisation that infer sensitive or protected attributes such as race, political opinions, trade union membership, religious or philosophical beliefs, sex life, or sexual orientation. Does not include categorisation that is ancillary to another commercial service and strictly necessary for objective technical reasons (e.g., labelling or filtering of lawfully obtained biometric data sets).

**Examples**:
- Systems inferring ethnicity from facial features
- Systems categorising individuals by religious dress or symbols via biometric analysis
- Demographic classification systems used at scale in public spaces

### 1(c) — Emotion recognition systems
AI systems intended for emotion recognition in the workplace, educational institutions, or by law enforcement, border management, or the administration of justice.

**Examples**:
- Workplace productivity monitoring systems that assess employee emotional states
- Educational proctoring tools that evaluate student stress or engagement via facial expressions
- Interview analysis tools that score candidates based on detected emotions
- Law enforcement interrogation tools that assess truthfulness via micro-expressions

## Area 2: Critical Infrastructure

**Governing articles**: Articles 6(2), 9, 14, 15

### 2(a) — Safety components of critical infrastructure
AI systems intended to be used as safety components in the management and operation of critical digital infrastructure, road traffic, or in the supply of water, gas, heating, or electricity.

**Examples**:
- AI managing electricity grid load balancing and fault detection
- AI controlling water treatment plant operations
- AI managing gas pipeline pressure and flow
- AI traffic management systems controlling traffic signals
- AI monitoring and managing data centre cooling and power distribution
- AI systems managing railway signalling
- AI systems optimising district heating distribution

### 2(b) — Not further subdivided in the Regulation
(Area 2 contains only the single sub-point above in the final text.)

## Area 3: Education and Vocational Training

**Governing articles**: Articles 6(2), 9, 10, 13, 14, 26, 27

### 3(a) — Access and admission
AI systems intended to determine access to or admission into educational and vocational training institutions at all levels.

**Examples**:
- Automated university admissions scoring systems
- AI-driven school placement and allocation systems
- Vocational training programme eligibility screening tools
- Scholarship and financial aid decision systems

### 3(b) — Student evaluation and performance
AI systems intended to evaluate learning outcomes, including systems used to steer the learning process, and systems intended to assess the appropriate level of education for an individual.

**Examples**:
- Automated essay scoring and grading systems
- AI-driven adaptive learning platforms that determine student progression
- Systems deciding student remediation or advancement
- AI proctoring systems that flag cheating or irregular behaviour during exams

### 3(c) — Purpose of education and level of access
AI systems intended to assess the appropriate level of education that an individual will receive or be able to access, or to monitor and detect prohibited behaviour of students during tests.

**Examples**:
- AI systems that determine student tracking (e.g., academic vs. vocational track)
- Online exam proctoring AI that detects prohibited behaviour
- Systems recommending educational pathways based on student assessment data

## Area 4: Employment, Workers Management, and Access to Self-Employment

**Governing articles**: Articles 6(2), 9, 10, 13, 14, 26, 27

### 4(a) — Recruitment and selection
AI systems intended to be used for recruitment or selection of natural persons, in particular for placing targeted job advertisements, analysing and filtering job applications, and evaluating candidates.

**Examples**:
- Resume screening and ranking tools
- AI video interview analysis scoring candidates
- Job advertisement targeting systems that optimise candidate pools
- Chatbot-based initial candidate screening
- Predictive hiring tools estimating candidate success

### 4(b) — Decisions affecting terms of work
AI systems intended to make or materially influence decisions on promotion, termination of work-related contractual relationships, task allocation based on individual behaviour or personal traits or characteristics, and performance and behaviour monitoring and evaluation.

**Examples**:
- Automated employee performance scoring systems
- AI-driven task allocation in warehouse/logistics operations
- Promotion recommendation systems based on performance data
- Automated termination decision tools
- Worker productivity surveillance systems using keystroke, screen, or location monitoring
- Gig economy algorithmic management systems (rating, task assignment, deactivation)

---

## Area 5: Access to and Enjoyment of Essential Private Services and Essential Public Services and Benefits

**Governing articles**: Articles 6(2), 9, 14, 26, 27, 29

### 5(a) — Creditworthiness assessment
AI systems intended to evaluate the creditworthiness of natural persons or establish their credit score, with the exception of AI systems used for the purpose of detecting financial fraud.

**Examples**:
- Automated credit scoring models
- AI-driven loan approval/denial systems
- Alternative data credit assessment tools (using social media, phone usage, etc.)
- Buy-now-pay-later eligibility determination systems

### 5(b) — Risk assessment and pricing in life and health insurance
AI systems intended to be used for risk assessment and pricing in relation to natural persons in the case of life and health insurance.

**Examples**:
- AI underwriting systems for life insurance
- Health insurance premium calculation systems using predictive models
- Wearable/health data-driven insurance pricing tools
- Pre-existing condition risk assessment AI

### 5(c) — Evaluation and classification of emergency calls
AI systems intended to evaluate and classify emergency calls by natural persons or to be used to dispatch or establish priority for emergency first response services, including police, firefighters, and medical aid, as well as emergency healthcare patient triage systems.

**Examples**:
- AI-powered 112/911 call triage systems
- Automated emergency dispatch prioritisation tools
- Hospital emergency room AI triage systems
- Systems classifying emergency call severity and routing

### 5(d) — Public assistance benefits and services
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of public authorities to evaluate eligibility for public assistance benefits and services, including healthcare, and to grant, reduce, revoke, or reclaim such benefits and services.

**Examples**:
- Welfare benefit eligibility determination systems
- Social housing allocation AI
- Public healthcare service rationing or prioritisation systems
- Unemployment benefit fraud detection and eligibility verification AI

## Area 6: Law Enforcement

**Governing articles**: Articles 6(2), 5(1)(d)(h), 9, 14, 26, 27

Insofar as their use is permitted under relevant Union and Member State law.

### 6(a) — Individual risk assessment
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of law enforcement authorities, or by Union institutions, bodies, offices or agencies, to assess the risk of a natural person becoming the victim of criminal offences.

**Examples**:
- Domestic violence risk assessment tools
- Victim vulnerability scoring systems
- AI predicting likelihood of re-victimisation

### 6(b) — Polygraphs and similar tools
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of law enforcement authorities as polygraphs or similar tools to detect the emotional state of natural persons.

**Examples**:
- AI-enhanced lie detection systems
- Micro-expression analysis during police interviews
- Voice stress analysis tools used in investigations

### 6(c) — Deep fakes detection
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of law enforcement authorities to detect deep fakes as referred to in Article 50(4).

**Examples**:
- Forensic AI tools authenticating video evidence
- Systems detecting manipulated audio recordings submitted as evidence

### 6(d) — Evaluation of reliability of evidence
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of law enforcement authorities for evaluating the reliability of evidence in the course of investigation or prosecution of criminal offences.

**Examples**:
- AI-assisted evidence reliability scoring
- Digital forensics AI evaluating chain of custody integrity
- Automated assessment of witness statement consistency

### 6(e) — Predicting occurrence or reoccurrence of criminal offences
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of law enforcement authorities for predicting the occurrence or reoccurrence of an actual or potential criminal offence based on profiling of natural persons, or for assessing personality traits, characteristics, or past criminal behaviour. Note: this does not include AI systems used to support the human assessment of the involvement of a person in criminal activity based on objective and verifiable facts.

**Examples**:
- Recidivism prediction tools (COMPAS-style systems)
- Predictive crime mapping systems that profile individuals
- Offender risk assessment tools used in parole/probation decisions

### 6(f) — Crime analytics regarding natural persons
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of law enforcement authorities for profiling of natural persons in the course of detection, investigation, or prosecution of criminal offences.

**Examples**:
- Suspect identification and prioritisation systems
- AI-driven criminal network analysis tools profiling individuals
- Social media analysis tools identifying persons of interest

## Area 7: Migration, Asylum, and Border Control Management

**Governing articles**: Articles 6(2), 9, 14, 26, 27, Annex IX

### 7(a) — Polygraphs and similar tools
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of competent public authorities or by Union institutions, bodies, offices or agencies as polygraphs or similar tools to assess the risk posed by natural persons entering the territory of a Member State or applying for a visa or asylum.

**Examples**:
- Automated deception detection at border crossings
- AI stress analysis of asylum interview responses
- Emotional state assessment during visa interviews

### 7(b) — Risk assessment
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of competent public authorities or by Union institutions, bodies, offices or agencies to assess a risk (including a security risk, a risk of irregular migration, or a health risk) posed by a natural person who intends to enter or has entered the territory of a Member State.

**Examples**:
- Automated border risk-scoring systems
- AI visa application risk assessment tools
- Immigration fraud detection systems
- Passenger pre-screening systems (connecting to large-scale IT systems in Annex IX)

### 7(c) — Examination of applications
AI systems intended to assist competent public authorities in the examination of applications for asylum, visa, and residence permits, and associated complaints regarding the eligibility of natural persons.

**Examples**:
- Automated document analysis for asylum applications
- AI-assisted country-of-origin information assessment
- Visa application processing and decision-support systems
- Residence permit eligibility screening tools

### 7(d) — Detection, recognition, or identification of persons
AI systems intended to be used by or on behalf of competent public authorities or by Union institutions for the purpose of detection, recognition, or identification of natural persons, with the exception of verification of travel documents.

**Examples**:
- Border surveillance AI identifying individuals crossing irregularly
- AI systems identifying persons at ports of entry beyond document verification
- Facial recognition at immigration checkpoints (not simple document-face matching)

## Area 8: Administration of Justice and Democratic Processes

**Governing articles**: Articles 6(2), 9, 14, 26, 27

### 8(a) — Judicial decision-making
AI systems intended to be used by a judicial authority or on their behalf to assist in researching and interpreting facts and the law, and in applying the law to a concrete set of facts, or to be used in alternative dispute resolution.

**Examples**:
- AI legal research tools recommending relevant case law to judges
- Sentencing recommendation systems
- AI-driven alternative dispute resolution platforms
- Automated small claims decision systems
- Predictive justice tools estimating case outcomes for judicial use

### 8(b) — Influence on elections and referenda
AI systems intended to be used for influencing the outcome of an election or referendum, or the voting behaviour of natural persons in the exercise of their vote in elections or referenda. This does not include AI systems to which natural persons are not directly exposed, such as tools used to organise, optimise, and structure political campaigns from an administrative and logistical point of view.

**Examples**:
- AI-driven political micro-targeting systems showing personalised political content to influence votes
- Deepfake generation tools used for political campaign material directed at voters
- AI chatbots engaging directly with voters to influence their voting decisions

Note: Campaign management tools (logistics, scheduling, volunteer coordination) that do not directly target or interact with voters are excluded.

## Article 6(3) Exception

A system listed in Annex III is **not** classified as high-risk if it does not pose a significant risk of harm to the health, safety, or fundamental rights of natural persons, including by not materially influencing the outcome of decision-making. This exception does **not** apply if the AI system performs profiling of natural persons as defined in Article 4(4) of Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR).

Providers relying on this exception must document the assessment before placing the system on the market and register it in the EU database under Article 49(2).
