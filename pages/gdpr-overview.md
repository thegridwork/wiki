# GDPR Overview

> The General Data Protection Regulation (EU) 2016/679 is the European Union's comprehensive data protection law, consisting of 11 chapters and 99 articles, with extraterritorial reach and enforcement powers including fines up to 4% of global annual turnover.

## Key Points
- Adopted April 14, 2016; enforceable from May 25, 2018
- Replaces the Data Protection Directive 95/46/EC
- Applies to any organization processing personal data of individuals in the EEA, regardless of where the organization is established (Article 3)
- 11 chapters, 99 articles, 173 recitals
- Enforced by independent supervisory authorities (Data Protection Authorities / DPAs) in each EU/EEA member state
- Maximum fines: EUR 20 million or 4% of worldwide annual turnover, whichever is greater (Article 83)
- Directly applicable across all EU member states (no transposition required, unlike directives)

## Detail

### Structure of the Regulation

The GDPR is organized into 11 chapters:

**Chapter I -- General Provisions (Articles 1-4)**
Establishes the regulation's subject matter, material scope, territorial scope, and definitions. Article 4 defines 26 key terms including "personal data" (any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person), "processing" (any operation performed on personal data), "controller" (entity determining purposes and means of processing), and "processor" (entity processing data on behalf of a controller).

**Chapter II -- Principles (Articles 5-11)**
Sets out the foundational principles of data processing under Article 5: lawfulness, fairness, and transparency; purpose limitation; data minimisation; accuracy; storage limitation; integrity and confidentiality; and accountability. Article 6 establishes the six legal bases for lawful processing. Articles 7-8 address consent conditions including children's consent. Article 9 covers special categories of data (health, biometric, genetic, racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, sexual orientation, trade union membership).

**Chapter III -- Rights of the Data Subject (Articles 12-23)**
Enumerates the rights of individuals: access (Article 15), rectification (Article 16), erasure/right to be forgotten (Article 17), restriction of processing (Article 18), data portability (Article 20), right to object (Article 21), and protections against automated decision-making (Article 22). Article 12 establishes the requirement for transparent communication. Article 23 permits member state restrictions on these rights under defined conditions.

**Chapter IV -- Controller and Processor (Articles 24-43)**
Covers obligations of controllers and processors including data protection by design and by default (Article 25), processor agreements (Article 28), records of processing activities (Article 30), security of processing (Article 32), breach notification (Articles 33-34), data protection impact assessments (Article 35), and Data Protection Officer requirements (Articles 37-39). Also addresses codes of conduct (Articles 40-41) and certification (Articles 42-43).

**Chapter V -- Transfers to Third Countries (Articles 44-50)**
Governs international data transfers outside the EEA. Mechanisms include adequacy decisions (Article 45), standard contractual clauses and binding corporate rules (Articles 46-47), and derogations (Article 49).

**Chapter VI -- Independent Supervisory Authorities (Articles 51-59)**
Establishes the framework for DPAs, their independence, competence, powers, and tasks.

**Chapter VII -- Cooperation and Consistency (Articles 60-76)**
Creates the cooperation and consistency mechanism between DPAs, the one-stop-shop mechanism (Article 60), mutual assistance (Article 61), and the European Data Protection Board (Articles 68-76).

**Chapter VIII -- Remedies, Liability, and Penalties (Articles 77-84)**
Provides for the right to lodge a complaint with a DPA (Article 77), judicial remedies (Articles 78-79), representation by not-for-profit bodies (Article 80), right to compensation (Article 82), and administrative fines (Article 83).

**Chapter IX -- Specific Processing Situations (Articles 85-91)**
Addresses processing in relation to freedom of expression (Article 85), public access to documents (Article 86), national identification numbers (Article 87), employment context (Article 88), archiving/research/statistics (Article 89), secrecy obligations (Article 90), and churches (Article 91).

**Chapters X-XI -- Delegated Acts and Final Provisions (Articles 92-99)**
Administrative and transitional provisions. Article 99 confirms the entry into force date (May 24, 2016) and application date (May 25, 2018).

### Territorial Scope (Article 3)

The GDPR has extraterritorial reach. It applies to:

1. **Establishment in the EU** (Article 3(1)): Processing carried out in the context of the activities of an establishment of a controller or processor in the EU, regardless of whether processing takes place in the EU.
2. **Targeting EU individuals** (Article 3(2)): Processing of personal data of data subjects who are in the EU by a controller or processor not established in the EU, where activities relate to offering goods or services to such data subjects (whether or not payment is required) or monitoring their behaviour within the EU.

This means any website, app, or service accessible to EU users that processes their personal data is potentially within scope.

### Enforcement Structure

Each EU/EEA member state has at least one independent DPA. The European Data Protection Board (EDPB) coordinates enforcement across borders. The "one-stop-shop" mechanism (Article 56) designates a lead supervisory authority for cross-border processing cases, determined by the location of the controller's main establishment.

The EDPB issues guidelines, opinions, and binding decisions to ensure consistent application of the GDPR across member states.

### Relationship to Other Instruments

The GDPR works alongside several other legal instruments:
- **ePrivacy Directive (2002/58/EC)**: Governs electronic communications privacy, including cookie consent rules (Article 5(3)). The proposed ePrivacy Regulation would replace it but remains stalled.
- **Law Enforcement Directive (EU) 2016/680**: Covers data protection in criminal justice contexts.
- **National implementing laws**: While directly applicable, the GDPR contains numerous "opening clauses" allowing member states to specify or restrict certain provisions (e.g., age of consent for children, employee data processing).
