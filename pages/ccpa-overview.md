# CCPA/CPRA Overview

> The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), as amended by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA), is California's comprehensive consumer data privacy law governing how for-profit businesses collect, use, sell, and share the personal information of California residents.

## Key Points
- CCPA enacted June 28, 2018 (AB 375), effective January 1, 2020; CPRA passed November 3, 2020 (Proposition 24), operative January 1, 2023
- Applies to for-profit entities doing business in California meeting any one of three thresholds: $25M revenue, 100,000+ consumers/households data, or 50%+ revenue from data sales
- Establishes six consumer rights: know, delete, correct, opt-out of sale/sharing, limit sensitive PI use, and non-discrimination
- CPRA created the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) as an independent enforcement body, replacing the AG's primary enforcement role
- CPRA eliminated the 30-day cure period for CPPA enforcement actions
- Penalties: $2,500 per unintentional violation, $7,500 per intentional violation or violation involving minors under 16

## Detail

### Structure and Codification

The CCPA is codified in the California Civil Code, Division 3, Part 4, Title 1.81.5, sections 1798.100 through 1798.199. The law comprises provisions on consumer rights (ss 1798.100-1798.125), business obligations and compliance procedures (ss 1798.130-1798.135), definitions (s 1798.140), exemptions (s 1798.145), enforcement (ss 1798.150-1798.160), and agency structure (ss 1798.196-1798.199).

### Applicability Thresholds

A for-profit entity doing business in California is subject to the CCPA if it meets any one of three thresholds (s 1798.140):

1. **Revenue threshold:** Annual gross revenue exceeding $25,000,000
2. **Data volume threshold:** Buys, sells, or shares personal information of 100,000 or more consumers or households per year (CPRA raised this from the original 50,000)
3. **Data revenue threshold:** Derives 50% or more of annual revenue from selling or sharing consumers' personal information

### Exemptions

The CCPA exempts HIPAA-covered entities (for health data), GLBA-covered entities (for financial data), FCRA-covered data, nonprofits, and government agencies (s 1798.145). Employment data was subject to a transitional exemption that expired January 1, 2023.

### CPRA Amendments

The CPRA, passed by California voters as Proposition 24 in November 2020, made significant amendments:

- Added the right to correct inaccurate personal information (s 1798.106)
- Added the right to limit use of sensitive personal information (s 1798.121)
- Expanded "sale" to include "sharing" for cross-context behavioral advertising (s 1798.140)
- Introduced data minimization requirements
- Created contractor obligations with audit rights
- Established the CPPA as an independent enforcement agency (s 1798.196)
- Eliminated the 30-day cure period for CPPA enforcement
- Required risk assessments and cybersecurity audits via rulemaking (s 1798.185)
- CPRA lookback date: personal information collected on or after January 1, 2022 is subject to expanded rights

### CPPA Enforcement

The California Privacy Protection Agency (s 1798.196) is an independent state agency governed by a five-member board appointed by the Governor, Attorney General, Senate Rules Committee, and Speaker of the Assembly. Its powers include investigating violations, issuing subpoenas, bringing civil actions with administrative fines, adopting regulations, conducting cybersecurity audits, and requiring risk assessments for high-risk processing. CPPA enforcement authority began July 1, 2023.

### Private Right of Action

Section 1798.150 provides a limited private right of action for data breaches involving nonencrypted or nonredacted personal information due to a business's failure to implement reasonable security measures. Statutory damages range from $100 to $750 per consumer per incident, or actual damages, whichever is greater. A 30-day written notice is required before filing suit.
