# GDPR Data Subject Rights

> Articles 12-22 of the GDPR grant individuals a comprehensive set of rights over their personal data, including access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, objection, and protection against automated decision-making, with controllers required to respond within one month.

## Key Points
- Eight distinct rights codified in Articles 15-22
- Article 12 establishes the overarching framework: responses must be concise, transparent, intelligible, in plain language, and free of charge
- Standard response deadline: one month from receipt of request, extendable by two additional months for complex or numerous requests (Article 12(3))
- Controllers must verify the identity of the requester but cannot use this as an excuse to delay or refuse
- Rights are not absolute -- Article 23 permits member state restrictions for national security, defense, public security, crime prevention, and other defined purposes
- Failure to facilitate rights is one of the most commonly enforced GDPR violations

## Detail

### Article 12 -- Transparent Information and Communication

Article 12 is the procedural backbone for all data subject rights. It requires controllers to:

- Provide information in a **concise, transparent, intelligible, and easily accessible form** using clear and plain language, particularly for information addressed to children
- Facilitate the exercise of rights under Articles 15-22
- Respond **without undue delay** and within **one month** of receipt
- Extend the deadline by **two further months** where necessary, considering the complexity and number of requests, but must inform the data subject of the extension and reasons within one month
- Provide responses **free of charge**; may charge a reasonable fee for manifestly unfounded or excessive requests, or refuse to act (Article 12(5))
- Provide information in writing or by electronic means where the request was made electronically (Article 12(1))

### Article 13 -- Information Provided at Collection

When personal data is collected directly from the data subject, the controller must provide at the time of collection:
- Identity and contact details of the controller (and DPO if applicable)
- Purposes and legal basis for processing
- Legitimate interests pursued (if relying on Article 6(1)(f))
- Recipients or categories of recipients
- Intent to transfer to a third country and the safeguard mechanism
- Retention period or criteria for determining it
- Existence of each data subject right applicable
- Right to withdraw consent (if consent is the legal basis)
- Right to lodge a complaint with a DPA
- Whether provision of data is a statutory/contractual requirement and consequences of non-provision
- Existence of automated decision-making including profiling (Article 22) with meaningful information about the logic, significance, and envisaged consequences

### Article 14 -- Information When Data Not Obtained From Data Subject

Similar to Article 13 but applies when data is obtained from third parties. Must additionally provide the categories of personal data obtained and the source. Must be provided within a reasonable period (at most one month), at first communication, or when data is first disclosed to another recipient.

### Article 15 -- Right of Access

The data subject has the right to obtain from the controller:
- Confirmation of whether personal data is being processed
- Access to the personal data itself
- Information about purposes, categories of data, recipients, retention period, rights available, source of data (if not collected from the subject), and automated decision-making details
- Where data is transferred to a third country, information about appropriate safeguards (Article 46)
- The controller must provide a **copy of the personal data** undergoing processing, free of charge for the first copy (Article 15(3)). Additional copies may incur a reasonable fee.

**Practical notes:** Access requests (also called Subject Access Requests / SARs) are the most commonly exercised right. They can be resource-intensive for controllers. The right must not adversely affect the rights and freedoms of others (Article 15(4)).

### Article 16 -- Right to Rectification

The data subject has the right to obtain rectification of inaccurate personal data without undue delay. Considering the purposes of processing, the data subject also has the right to have incomplete data completed, including by means of a supplementary statement.

### Article 17 -- Right to Erasure (Right to Be Forgotten)

The data subject has the right to erasure where:
- Data is no longer necessary for its original purpose (Article 17(1)(a))
- Consent is withdrawn and no other legal basis exists (Article 17(1)(b))
- The data subject objects under Article 21 and there are no overriding legitimate grounds (Article 17(1)(c))
- Data has been unlawfully processed (Article 17(1)(d))
- Erasure is required by EU or member state law (Article 17(1)(e))
- Data was collected in relation to the offer of information society services to a child (Article 17(1)(f))

**Exceptions (Article 17(3)):** Erasure does not apply where processing is necessary for exercising freedom of expression, compliance with a legal obligation, public health purposes, archiving in the public interest / research / statistics, or the establishment/exercise/defence of legal claims.

Where the controller has made data public, it must take reasonable steps to inform other controllers processing the data that erasure has been requested (Article 17(2)).

### Article 18 -- Right to Restriction of Processing

The data subject may request restriction (data is stored but not otherwise processed) where:
- Accuracy is contested -- restriction for the period needed to verify
- Processing is unlawful but the data subject opposes erasure
- The controller no longer needs the data but the data subject requires it for legal claims
- The data subject has objected under Article 21 -- restriction pending verification of whether the controller's legitimate grounds override

The controller must inform the data subject before lifting any restriction.

### Article 19 -- Notification Obligation

The controller must communicate any rectification, erasure, or restriction of processing to each recipient the data was disclosed to, unless this proves impossible or involves disproportionate effort. The controller must inform the data subject about these recipients if requested.

### Article 20 -- Right to Data Portability

The data subject has the right to receive personal data they provided to a controller in a **structured, commonly used, and machine-readable format**, and to transmit that data to another controller without hindrance, where:
- Processing is based on consent (Article 6(1)(a) or Article 9(2)(a)) or contract (Article 6(1)(b)), AND
- Processing is carried out by automated means

Where technically feasible, the data subject may request direct transmission from one controller to another (Article 20(2)). This right must not adversely affect the rights and freedoms of others.

### Article 21 -- Right to Object

**General objection (Article 21(1)):** The data subject may object to processing based on public task (Article 6(1)(e)) or legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f)), including profiling based on those provisions. The controller must cease processing unless it demonstrates compelling legitimate grounds that override the data subject's interests, rights, and freedoms, or for the establishment, exercise, or defence of legal claims.

**Direct marketing (Article 21(2-3)):** Where personal data is processed for direct marketing purposes, the data subject has an **absolute right to object** at any time. No balancing test applies. Processing must cease upon objection. This right must be explicitly brought to the data subject's attention, clearly and separately from other information, at the latest at the time of first communication.

**Research/statistics (Article 21(6)):** The data subject may object to processing for scientific/historical research or statistical purposes, unless processing is necessary for a task carried out in the public interest.

### Article 22 -- Automated Decision-Making and Profiling

The data subject has the right not to be subject to a decision based **solely** on automated processing, including profiling, which **produces legal effects** or **similarly significantly affects** them.

**Exceptions (Article 22(2)):** This does not apply where the decision is:
- Necessary for entering into or performing a contract
- Authorized by EU or member state law with suitable safeguards
- Based on the data subject's explicit consent

In the exception cases, the controller must implement suitable safeguards including at minimum the right to obtain human intervention, to express their point of view, and to contest the decision (Article 22(3)).

Special category data may only be used in automated decision-making with explicit consent or substantial public interest, with suitable safeguards (Article 22(4)).

### Article 23 -- Restrictions

Member states may restrict the scope of rights under Articles 12-22 (and corresponding Article 5 principles) through legislative measures where the restriction is necessary and proportionate to safeguard: national security, defence, public security, crime prevention/prosecution, other important public interests, judicial independence, regulatory/supervisory functions, protection of the data subject or the rights/freedoms of others, or enforcement of civil law claims.

Such legislative measures must contain specific provisions on: the purposes of the processing, the categories of personal data, the scope of restrictions, safeguards against abuse, the controller(s), storage periods, and risks to data subject rights/freedoms.

### Response Timeframes Summary

| Action | Timeframe |
|--------|-----------|
| Standard response | 1 month from receipt |
| Extension for complex/numerous requests | +2 months (notify within 1 month) |
| Refusal notification | Within 1 month, with reasons and right to complain to DPA |
| Restriction notification (Article 14) | Within 1 month, at first communication, or at first disclosure |

## Product Relevance
- **privacy-mcp**: Privacy policy analysis checks whether the policy informs data subjects of all applicable rights (a requirement under Articles 13(2)(b-d) and 14(2)(c-e)). Missing rights disclosures in a privacy policy are a compliance finding. The consent audit verifies that the right to withdraw consent is communicated. Data collection checks assess whether the organization's data practices are consistent with enabling portability and erasure.
- **siteaudit-mcp**: Cookie banners may need to reference the right to withdraw consent. Accessibility of rights-exercise mechanisms (e.g., contact forms for SARs) is tangentially relevant.
