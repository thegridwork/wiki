# GDPR Privacy Policy Requirements

> Articles 13-14 of the GDPR prescribe an exhaustive list of information that must be provided to data subjects about how their personal data is processed, forming the legal basis for what is commonly known as a "privacy policy" or "privacy notice," with transparency requirements under Article 12 governing how that information must be communicated.

## Key Points
- Articles 13 and 14 list mandatory disclosure items -- a privacy policy missing any of them is non-compliant
- Article 13 applies when data is collected directly from the data subject; Article 14 applies when data is obtained from other sources
- Article 12 requires information to be concise, transparent, intelligible, easily accessible, in clear and plain language
- The most commonly violated transparency requirements (per enforcement data): purpose specification, legal basis identification, retention periods, third-party recipients, and international transfer disclosures
- WhatsApp was fined EUR 405 million (2022) primarily for transparency violations under Articles 13-14
- This is the core knowledge base for privacy-mcp's privacy policy auditor

## Detail

### Article 12 -- Transparent Communication

Article 12 is the overarching framework for how privacy information must be communicated:

**Article 12(1):** The controller must take appropriate measures to provide information under Articles 13-14 and communications under Articles 15-22 and 34 in a:
- **Concise** form -- not excessively long or detailed
- **Transparent** manner -- no hidden or buried clauses
- **Intelligible** format -- understandable to the intended audience
- **Easily accessible** way -- not behind multiple clicks or paywalls
- **Clear and plain language** -- particularly when addressed to a child

Information may be provided in writing (including electronically) or by other means, including, where appropriate, visually (icons, layered notices, dashboards). If requested by the data subject, information may be provided orally (provided identity is verified).

**Article 12(5):** Information under Articles 13-14 is provided free of charge. Excessive or manifestly unfounded requests may be charged a reasonable fee or refused.

**Article 12(7):** The Commission may specify standardized icons to provide information in an easily visible, intelligible, and clearly legible manner (these have not yet been adopted as of 2026).

### Article 13 -- Information When Data Collected From the Data Subject

When personal data is collected **directly from the data subject** (e.g., via forms, account creation, cookies on a website), the controller must provide the following **at the time the data is obtained:**

#### Mandatory Disclosures -- Article 13(1):

1. **Identity and contact details of the controller** (and where applicable, the controller's representative)
2. **Contact details of the Data Protection Officer** (where applicable)
3. **Purposes of the processing** and the **legal basis** for processing (Article 6)
4. **Legitimate interests** pursued by the controller or a third party (where Article 6(1)(f) is the legal basis)
5. **Recipients or categories of recipients** of the personal data
6. **Intention to transfer data to a third country** or international organization, the existence or absence of an adequacy decision, or reference to the appropriate safeguards (Article 46) or derogations (Article 49), and the means by which to obtain a copy of the safeguards or where they have been made available

#### Mandatory Disclosures -- Article 13(2):

To ensure fair and transparent processing, the controller must additionally provide at the time of collection:

7. **Retention period** or the criteria used to determine the retention period
8. **Existence of data subject rights:** right to access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, and objection
9. **Right to withdraw consent** at any time (where consent is the legal basis), without affecting the lawfulness of processing before withdrawal
10. **Right to lodge a complaint** with a supervisory authority
11. **Whether the provision of personal data is a statutory or contractual requirement**, or a requirement necessary to enter into a contract, and the consequences of failure to provide the data
12. **Existence of automated decision-making, including profiling** (Article 22(1) and (4)), and meaningful information about the logic involved, the significance, and the envisaged consequences of such processing for the data subject

### Article 14 -- Information When Data Not Obtained From the Data Subject

When personal data is obtained from **third parties** (data brokers, public sources, other controllers, partners), the same information must be provided plus:

13. **Categories of personal data** obtained (since the controller didn't collect them directly, the subject needs to know what was obtained)
14. **The source** from which the personal data originates, and whether it came from publicly accessible sources

**Timing (Article 14(3)):** Information must be provided:
- Within a **reasonable period** after obtaining the data (no later than one month)
- If the data is to be used for communication with the data subject, at the latest at the time of the **first communication**
- If disclosure to another recipient is envisaged, at the latest when the data is **first disclosed**

**Exceptions (Article 14(5)):** Article 14 does not apply where:
- The data subject already has the information
- Provision of the information would be impossible or involve disproportionate effort (particularly for processing for archiving, research, or statistical purposes) -- but alternative measures must be taken (making information publicly available)
- Obtaining or disclosure is expressly required by law
- The data is subject to professional secrecy

### Practical Checklist for a Compliant Privacy Policy

Based on Articles 13-14, a complete privacy policy should contain:

| Item | Article | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Controller identity and contact details | 13(1)(a) / 14(1)(a) | Legal name, address, email/phone |
| DPO contact details | 13(1)(b) / 14(1)(b) | If a DPO is appointed |
| Purposes of processing | 13(1)(c) / 14(1)(c) | Each distinct purpose |
| Legal basis for each purpose | 13(1)(c) / 14(1)(c) | Must specify which Article 6(1) basis applies |
| Legitimate interests (if applicable) | 13(1)(d) / 14(2)(b) | Identify the specific interests pursued |
| Recipients or categories | 13(1)(e) / 14(1)(e) | Third-party services, analytics providers, ad networks |
| International transfer details | 13(1)(f) / 14(1)(f) | Countries, safeguard mechanisms (SCCs, adequacy, DPF) |
| Retention period or criteria | 13(2)(a) / 14(2)(a) | Specific periods or clear criteria |
| Data subject rights | 13(2)(b) / 14(2)(c) | Access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, objection |
| Right to withdraw consent | 13(2)(c) / 14(2)(d) | If consent is a legal basis |
| Right to complain to DPA | 13(2)(d) / 14(2)(e) | Including how to contact the relevant DPA |
| Whether data provision is statutory/contractual | 13(2)(e) | And consequences of not providing |
| Automated decision-making / profiling | 13(2)(f) / 14(2)(g) | Logic, significance, envisaged consequences |
| Categories of data obtained | 14(1)(d) | Only for Article 14 (third-party sourced data) |
| Source of data | 14(2)(f) | Only for Article 14, including whether publicly accessible |

### Common Compliance Failures

Based on enforcement actions and DPA guidance, the most frequently identified deficiencies in privacy policies are:

1. **Vague or missing legal bases:** Stating "we process your data for our business purposes" without identifying which Article 6(1) basis applies to each purpose.

2. **Incomplete recipient disclosure:** Failing to list categories of third-party services receiving data (analytics, advertising, payment processors, hosting providers). Generic statements like "we may share data with third parties" are insufficient.

3. **Missing or vague retention periods:** Stating "we retain data as long as necessary" without specifying actual periods or meaningful criteria.

4. **Absent transfer disclosures:** Failing to disclose that data is transferred outside the EEA (e.g., to US-based services) and the safeguard mechanism relied upon.

5. **Bundled or inaccessible policies:** Privacy information buried in terms of service, requiring extensive scrolling, or using legal jargon incomprehensible to the average person.

6. **Missing automated decision-making disclosure:** Failing to inform data subjects about profiling or algorithmic decisions that affect them.

7. **Outdated policies:** Not updating policies when processing activities change, new third-party services are added, or legal bases shift.

8. **Missing right to withdraw:** Where consent is a legal basis, failing to mention that it can be withdrawn and how.

### Layered Approach

The Article 29 Working Party (WP260rev.01 on Transparency) endorsed a "layered" approach to privacy information:

- **First layer:** Short notice at the point of data collection containing the most essential information (identity, purposes, rights summary, link to full policy)
- **Second layer:** Full privacy policy containing all Article 13/14 requirements
- **Just-in-time notices:** Contextual information provided at the moment a specific processing activity occurs (e.g., before enabling location tracking, before sharing data with a new partner)

This approach addresses the tension between comprehensiveness (all required information) and accessibility (concise, understandable).

### EDPB Guidance on Transparency

Key interpretive points from EDPB and WP29 guidance:

- The privacy policy must be **proactively provided** -- it is the controller's obligation, not the data subject's responsibility to find it
- **Privacy dashboards** and interactive tools are encouraged for complex processing environments
- Information must be in the **language of the data subject** -- a website targeting French users must provide a French-language privacy policy
- **Updates to privacy policies** should be communicated to existing users, not merely silently published
- Catch-all phrases ("including but not limited to," "we may," "among others") undermine the specificity requirement
