# GDPR Enforcement and Fines

> Article 83 of the GDPR establishes a two-tier fine structure with maximums of EUR 10 million / 2% of global turnover and EUR 20 million / 4% of global turnover, with DPAs having collectively imposed billions of euros in fines since 2018, most frequently for violations of data transfer rules, consent requirements, and transparency obligations.

## Key Points
- Two-tier fine structure: lower tier up to EUR 10M or 2% global turnover; upper tier up to EUR 20M or 4% global turnover (Article 83)
- Fines must be "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" in each individual case (Article 83(1))
- The largest GDPR fine to date is Meta's EUR 1.2 billion (Ireland DPC, 2023) for unlawful data transfers to the US
- The most commonly violated provisions involve transparency (Articles 13-14), legal basis/consent (Articles 5-6), and data security (Article 32)
- DPAs have additional corrective powers beyond fines: warnings, reprimands, orders to comply, processing bans, and data erasure orders (Article 58(2))
- The one-stop-shop mechanism has created enforcement bottlenecks, particularly with the Irish DPC handling cases for most major tech companies

## Detail

### Fine Structure (Article 83)

#### Lower Tier -- Article 83(4)
Infringements of the following provisions are subject to fines of up to **EUR 10 million** or **2% of total worldwide annual turnover** of the preceding financial year, whichever is higher:

- Obligations of the controller and processor (Articles 8, 11, 25-39, 42-43)
- This covers: children's consent conditions, data protection by design, processor agreements, records of processing, security of processing, breach notification, DPIAs, DPO requirements, codes of conduct, and certification

#### Upper Tier -- Article 83(5)
Infringements of the following provisions are subject to fines of up to **EUR 20 million** or **4% of total worldwide annual turnover**, whichever is higher:

- Basic principles for processing, including conditions for consent (Articles 5, 6, 7, 9)
- Data subjects' rights (Articles 12-22)
- International transfers (Articles 44-49)
- Member state law provisions adopted under Chapter IX
- Non-compliance with a DPA order (Article 58(2))

#### Fine Determination Criteria (Article 83(2))

When deciding whether to impose a fine and the amount, DPAs must consider:

**(a)** Nature, gravity, and duration of the infringement, number of data subjects affected, and level of damage suffered
**(b)** Intentional or negligent character of the infringement
**(c)** Actions taken to mitigate the damage
**(d)** Degree of responsibility, considering technical and organizational measures under Articles 25 and 32
**(e)** Any relevant previous infringements
**(f)** Degree of cooperation with the DPA
**(g)** Categories of personal data affected
**(h)** How the DPA became aware -- whether the controller/processor notified the infringement
**(i)** Compliance with previously ordered measures (Article 58(2))
**(j)** Adherence to codes of conduct or certification mechanisms
**(k)** Any other aggravating or mitigating factor, including financial benefits gained or losses avoided

### Enforcement Trends

**Most commonly violated articles (by frequency of enforcement):**

1. **Article 5 (Processing principles)** -- especially lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, and data minimisation. This is the most cited article in enforcement decisions because it underpins all processing.

2. **Article 6 (Legal basis)** -- failure to identify a valid legal basis, relying on the wrong basis, or failing to meet the conditions for the chosen basis (particularly consent and legitimate interests).

3. **Articles 13-14 (Transparency/information)** -- inadequate or incomplete privacy policies, failure to inform data subjects of all required elements, information not provided in clear and plain language.

4. **Article 32 (Security of processing)** -- inadequate technical and organizational security measures leading to data breaches. This includes insufficient encryption, poor access controls, lack of monitoring, and failure to implement security by design.

5. **Article 5(1)(f) read with Article 32 (Integrity and confidentiality)** -- the principle of appropriate security combined with the specific security obligation.

6. **Articles 44-49 (International transfers)** -- particularly post-Schrems II, with the massive Meta EUR 1.2B fine highlighting this area.

7. **Article 7 (Consent conditions)** -- consent mechanisms that fail to meet the GDPR standard: pre-ticked boxes, bundled consent, inadequate withdrawal mechanisms.

**Emerging enforcement areas (2024-2026):**
- **AI and automated decision-making:** DPAs increasingly scrutinizing AI systems for compliance with Articles 22 (automated decision-making), 35 (DPIA), and 5(1)(a) (lawfulness/transparency). The Italian Garante's temporary ban on ChatGPT in 2023 signaled this direction.
- **Dark patterns in consent:** Cookie banners designed to nudge users toward acceptance ("deceptive design patterns") are increasingly targeted. The EDPB's Guidelines 03/2022 on dark patterns specifically address this.
- **Children's data:** Following TikTok (EUR 345M) and Instagram (EUR 405M) fines, protection of minors' data is a top enforcement priority.
- **Real-time bidding (RTB) and adtech:** The Belgian DPA's 2022 decision against IAB Europe's Transparency and Consent Framework highlighted systemic issues with how programmatic advertising obtains and processes consent signals.

### Corrective Powers Beyond Fines (Article 58(2))

DPAs have a range of corrective powers:
- **(a)** Issue warnings about intended processing likely to infringe
- **(b)** Issue reprimands for infringements
- **(c)** Order the controller/processor to comply with data subject requests
- **(d)** Order processing to be brought into compliance in a specified manner and period
- **(e)** Order communication of a breach to data subjects
- **(f)** Impose a temporary or definitive limitation on processing, including a ban
- **(g)** Order rectification, restriction, or erasure of data and notification thereof to recipients
- **(h)** Withdraw a certification
- **(i)** Impose administrative fines
- **(j)** Order suspension of data flows to a third country recipient

The Irish DPC's suspension of Meta's EU-US data transfers (alongside the EUR 1.2B fine) demonstrates that non-monetary corrective powers can be equally or more impactful.

### One-Stop-Shop Challenges

The GDPR's one-stop-shop mechanism (Article 56) designates a lead DPA based on the controller's main establishment in the EU. This has concentrated enforcement against major tech companies with the Irish DPC, which has faced criticism for:
- Processing times: Major cases have taken 3-5 years from complaint to decision
- Fine levels: The EDPB has used its binding decision power (Article 65) to increase fines proposed by the Irish DPC in multiple cases (WhatsApp: EUR 225M increased to EUR 405M; Meta: fine order increased to EUR 1.2B)
- Scope of investigations: The EDPB has directed broader investigation scope in several cases

The dispute resolution mechanism under Article 65 has emerged as a de facto appellate layer, with the EDPB overruling or modifying lead DPA draft decisions in significant cases.

### Private Enforcement (Articles 77-82)

Beyond DPA enforcement, the GDPR enables private enforcement:
- **Article 77:** Right to lodge a complaint with a DPA
- **Article 78:** Right to judicial remedy against a DPA decision (or inaction)
- **Article 79:** Right to judicial remedy directly against a controller or processor
- **Article 80:** Representation by not-for-profit bodies (collective redress)
- **Article 82:** Right to compensation for material or non-material damage resulting from GDPR infringement

Class-action-style proceedings by consumer organizations (e.g., noyb, La Quadrature du Net, Verbraucherzentrale) are an increasingly significant enforcement vector.
